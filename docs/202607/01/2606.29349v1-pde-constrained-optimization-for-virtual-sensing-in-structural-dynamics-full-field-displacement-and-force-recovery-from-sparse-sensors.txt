Abstract 

Virtual sensing — recovering full-field structural response from sparse sensor measurements — is a fundamental challenge in structural health monitoring. This study formulates virtual sensing as a PDE-constrained optimization (PDE-CO) problem, where the governing elastodynamic equation serves as an equality constraint, the applied force distribution is the optimization variable, and the full-field displacement and force are jointly recovered. Gradients of the Tikhonov-regularized objective are computed via reverse-mode automatic di ff erentiation through the forward PDE solver, and L-BFGS iteratively finds the optimal state. The framework couples an o ffl ine FEniCSx stage for finite element assembly with an online GPU-accelerated JAX stage, and is verified on three examples of increasing complexity: a cantilever plate, a 90 ◦ elbow pipe, and a reactor pressure vessel (RPV) representative of a 300 MW pressurized nuclear reactor. PDE-CO consistently outperforms modal expansion across all three cases, reducing displacement errors by factors of 2 to 17 × with sub-percent accuracy on every example. Unlike modal expansion, where force is derived by back-calculation from truncated modal coordinates and is not jointly optimized, PDE-CO recovers displacement and force simultaneously through the PDE constraint; the increased computational cost is o ff set by GPU acceleration delivering up to 64 .8× speedup over CPU.

1. Introduction 

Virtual sensing — the recovery of full-field displacement, strain, and acceleration fields across an entire structure from sparse sensor measurements — is a key capability for structural health monitoring (SHM) (Ritto and Rochinha, 2021; Lee et al., 2023), fatigue assessment, and damage detection (Ge and Lui, 2005) (see Figure 1). The structural dynamics community has developed several approaches for virtual sensing, including modal least-squares regression (Lee et al., 2024), Kalman filter methods (Mora et al., 2023; Lourens et al., 2012), reduced-order modeling approaches (Oh et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2017, 2018), and physics-guided machine learning approaches (Haywood-Alexander et al., 2026). Among these, modal expansion is the most widely adopted: the displacement is represented as a linear combination of the Nd lowest-frequency (dominant) eigenmodes, and the modal coordinates are fitted to sensor data via least-squares. However, modal expansion su ff ers from fundamental limitations. First, retaining only Nd modes introduces modal truncation error, and Nd is bounded by the sensor count ( Nd ≤ Ns − 1 for an overdetermined system), so the sparse sensor data cannot constrain a higher-order modal basis. Second, the governing PDE is never enforced: the sensor data determines only Ns degrees of freedom, and no physical law propagates this information to the remaining N f − Ns DOFs. When Ns ≪ N f , this purely kinematic approach lacks the information to reconstruct the response accurately away from sensor locations. The scientific computing community, in parallel, has developed di ff erentiable simulation frameworks (Xue et al., 2023; Bezgin et al., 2023) that formulate inverse problems as PDE-constrained optimization (PDE-CO), with au-tomatic di ff erentiation handling all sensitivity computations. PDE-CO treats the governing equation as an equality constraint, ensuring the recovered solution is physically consistent at every optimization iteration. By enforcing the PDE, sparse sensor information is propagated to the entire domain through the physics, filling the information gap that modal expansion cannot bridge. The PDE-CO framework o ff ers several key advantages over modal expan-sion for virtual sensing. First, it eliminates modal truncation error. PDE-CO uses the full dynamic sti ff ness matrix 

D f f ∈ RN f ×N f rather than a truncated modal basis Φ ∈ RN f ×Nd , so the solution space is the entire N f -dimensional FE space. Second, the PDE constraint provides physics-based regularization. Every trial solution is consistent with the governing equation, and the inverse operator D−1 

> f f

acts as a smoothing operator that attenuates high-frequency spa-tial components. Third, gradients are computed via reverse-mode AD and supplied to L-BFGS, a unified paradigm that extends identically to nonlinear forward models, material identification, and topology optimization. Fourth, the full-field displacement emerges directly from the primal PDE solve at the optimal force, so both the displacement field and the force distribution are recovered simultaneously within a single optimization framework without auxiliary post-processing steps. Existing virtual sensing methods rely on modal expansion (Iliopoulos et al., 2016), Kalman filters (Lourens et al., 2012), physics-guided machine learning (Haywood-Alexander et al., 2026), or transfer function inversion, but do not exploit the full PDE constraint in a di ff erentiable optimization framework. 3. PDE-constrained optimization as an alternative 

The limitations identified in Section 2 — modal truncation error, absence of a physics constraint, and force re-covery failure — motivate an alternative framework. PDE-constrained optimization (PDE-CO) addresses all three by enforcing the governing equation as an equality constraint, operating on the full-order FE system without modal reduction, and recovering the unknown force distribution by minimizing the sensor data misfit.

Remark 6 (An important distinction from Dirichlet-type inverse problems). In many inverse problems, the un-known is a prescribed displacement on part of the boundary (Dirichlet BC), which requires partitioning into free, clamped, and prescribed sets. The Tikhonov term α∥θ∥2 in Eq. (40) plays di ff erent roles depending on the problem setting: 1. Underdetermined case (Nθ > Ns): The data misfit alone has infinitely many minimizers (a family of loads producing identical sensor readings). The regularization selects the minimum-norm solution by penalizing large force amplitudes. 2. Overdetermined case (Ns > Nθ): The system Aθ = ˆaobs is generically inconsistent (no exact solution exists, e.g., due to measurement noise). The regularization stabilizes the least-squares solution against ill-conditioning and noise amplification. 3. Noise-free, well-conditioned case : When the data is exact and A is well-conditioned, α can be set to a very small value (e.g., 10 −6) to provide minimal bias. The weight α controls the trade-o ff between data fidelity (small α, trust the data) and solution regularity (large α, pe-nalize large θ). In practice, α can be selected via the L-curve method, generalized cross-validation, or the discrepancy principle (Hansen, 1998).

7.1. Why PDE-CO achieves superior displacement recovery 

The numerical results across all three examples confirm the theoretical analysis of Section 4.7. PDE-CO achieves superior virtual sensing accuracy through two complementary mechanisms. First, the PDE constraint enforces exactness. The governing equation D f f ˆU f = Pθ ensures that, for any trial force

as a natural by-product of the optimization; while modal expansion can derive a force estimate by back-calculation (ˆ f = D f f Φq), this estimate inherits modal truncation error and is not jointly optimized with the displacement. This is particularly valuable for structural health monitoring scenarios where the loading acts on inaccessible surfaces, such as the inner surface of a pressure vessel. It should be noted that displacement accuracy is assessed quantitatively via DOF-wise error norms (Table 1), whereas load recovery is assessed qualitatively in this study. A direct scalar error norm for the recovered force is not reported because the optimization variable θ∗ is a distributed nodal force vector whose spatial distribution and sparsity di ff er fundamentally from the concentrated or Gaussian ground-truth load; comparing them as vectors would conflate spatial distribution di ff erences with amplitude errors. Instead, correctness of the recovered force is judged by whether the identified loading region and directional pattern match the known true load, which they do in all three examples. 

7.2. Why PDE-CO outperforms modal expansion 

The numerical results reveal an important nuance: modal expansion itself is a strong virtual sensing baseline. For the pipe (1 .11%) and RPV (1 .86%), modal expansion achieves sub-2% displacement errors that would be acceptable in many engineering applications, and the improvement brought by PDE-CO is meaningful but incremental in these cases. The picture is more dramatic for the thin plate (7 .69%), where modal truncation is severe due to the high-frequency bending modes that dominate the response near the clamped boundary. Displacement recovery, however, is only one facet of the comparison. PDE-CO’s distinctive advantage lies in load identification: the force θ is the primary optimization variable, jointly optimized with the displacement through the PDE constraint. Modal expansion can derive a force estimate by back-calculation (ˆ f = D f f Φq), but this estimate inherits modal truncation error and is not directly optimized against sensor data. This capability — qualitatively 22 verified in all three examples — is practically irreplaceable for structural health monitoring scenarios where the loading is unknown, inaccessible, or spatially distributed. Considering both criteria, the three examples yield displacement improvement ratios of 17 × for the plate (7 .69% →

0.45%), 4 .0× for the pipe (1 .11% → 0.28%), and 2 .1× for the RPV (1 .86% → 0.885%). The decreasing ratio from plate to RPV does not indicate that PDE-CO weakens; rather, it reflects that modal expansion performs less catastroph-ically as the sensor-to-mode ratio improves ( Nsensor /Nd = 12 /11 → 24 /23 → 108 /50) and as the structure becomes thick-walled (pipe, RPV) rather than thin (plate). Nonetheless, PDE-CO consistently achieves lower displacement error in absolute terms across all cases, including the RPV where the load acts on the inner surface — a scenario that modal expansion was never designed to handle. The underlying mechanisms are detailed in Section 7.1. Briefly, the modal truncation of the kinematic basis (Nd ≤ Ns − 1) directly limits displacement accuracy — most severely for the thin plate where high-frequency bending modes are discarded — while the absence of equilibrium enforcement renders any back-calculated force estimate dynamically inconsistent. PDE-CO eliminates both deficiencies through the governing equation constraint.

7.4. Limitations 

The current formulation rests on several assumptions that define its scope. The framework assumes linear elas-todynamics, so geometric or material nonlinearity is not addressed. The dynamic sti ff ness formulation is applicable to single-frequency harmonic loading with a known excitation frequency ω; broadband or transient loading requires extension to the time domain. Furthermore, the excitation frequency must be chosen away from the natural frequen-cies of the structure, since D f f (ω) becomes singular at resonance; the present study uses ω = ω1/2 for all examples, 23 and the sensitivity of the recovered solution to the choice of ω has not been systematically investigated. For large structural models with N f ≳ 10 4 DOFs, the dense LU factorization becomes computationally prohibitive, and sparse direct or iterative solvers must replace it. Finally, in the presence of significant measurement noise, the regularization parameter α must be selected carefully — for example, via the L-curve method or cross-validation — to balance data fidelity against solution regularity. 

7.5. Future extensions 

The di ff erentiable virtual sensing framework based on PDE-CO admits several natural extensions. Projection-based reduced-order models (ROMs) (Craig Jr and Bampton, 1968; Kim et al., 2017, 2018; Zahr and Farhat, 2014) can be incorporated to reduce the forward solve cost while retaining the physics constraint advantage, enabling ROM-accelerated PDE-CO for large-scale applications. Extension to the time domain via Newmark-β time integration would enable transient full-field inference and broaden applicability to impact and broadband loading scenarios. Multi-frequency and broadband problems can be addressed through superposition of harmonic solutions or frequency-domain sweeps. Geometric or material nonlinearity can be incorporated by reformulating the PDE constraint in resid-ual form, enabling force identification in structures where modal expansion is inapplicable. Finally, the di ff erentiable solver is directly compatible with Hamiltonian Monte Carlo sampling, enabling Bayesian inference for principled uncertainty quantification of both the recovered displacement field and the identified force distribution. 

8. Conclusions 

This study presented a PDE-constrained optimization (PDE-CO) framework for virtual sensing in structural dy-namics, recovering full-field displacement and applied loading from sparse acceleration sensor measurements. The framework couples an o ffl ine finite element stage (FEniCSx) with an online GPU-accelerated optimization stage (JAX), and was verified on three examples of increasing complexity: a cantilever plate, a 90 ◦ elbow pipe, and a double-loop cylindrical reactor pressure vessel (RPV) representative of a 300 MW pressurized nuclear reactor. PDE-CO consistently outperformed modal expansion in displacement recovery across all three examples. Modal expansion itself achieved respectable accuracy for the pipe ( ϵu = 1.11%) and RPV ( ϵu = 1.86%) where the sensor-to-mode ratio was favorable, but PDE-CO reduced these errors to 0 .28% and 0 .885%, respectively. The improvement was most dramatic for the thin cantilever plate, where modal truncation is severe and modal expansion yielded ϵu = 7.69%; PDE-CO reduced this to 0 .45%, a 17 × gain. Notably, despite a substantial increase in problem size from the plate (N f = 1,872) to the pipe ( N f = 11 ,160), the pipe exhibited lower error for both methods, reflecting the structural advantage of a thick-walled geometry where beam-bending modes dominate and modal truncation is less severe. The accuracy advantage of PDE-CO stems from the PDE constraint D f f ˆU f = Pθ, which enforces exact consis-tency with the full-order FE system. This eliminates modal truncation error and propagates sparse sensor information through the governing equation to the entire displacement field. The recovered displacement is therefore exactly dynamically consistent with the identified loading, regardless of the number or placement of sensors. Force identification is PDE-CO’s most distinctive advantage over modal expansion. While modal expansion can derive a force estimate through back-calculation from recovered modal coordinates (ˆ f = D f f Φq), this estimate inherits modal truncation error and is not jointly optimized with the displacement. PDE-CO simultaneously recovers the optimal force distribution f∗ = Pθ∗ as a natural by-product of the optimization. The recovered spatial loading pattern correctly identifies the loading region and directional character in all three examples, and for the RPV this extends to inner-surface load identification from outer-surface sensors alone — a virtual sensing capability with direct relevance to pressure vessel structural health monitoring. Load recovery is assessed qualitatively rather than by scalar error norms, since the di ff erence in spatial distribution between the recovered distributed force and the concentrated or Gaussian ground-truth load makes vector norm comparison an unsuitable quality metric. The dominant computational cost is the dense linear solve D f f x = b, called twice per L-BFGS iteration via JAX’s cuSOLVER backend. GPU acceleration on an NVIDIA RTX 5070 Ti provides up to 64 .8× speedup over CPU at 

N f ≈ 12 ,000, reducing the RPV per-iteration cost from ∼7.6 s to ∼0.15 s and making near-real-time monitoring feasible at the mesh sizes considered. However, as the number of force parameters Nθ increases substantially — for example, when the candidate loaded boundary is large or finely discretized — the L-BFGS convergence requires more iterations, and total optimization cost grows accordingly; GPU infrastructure is therefore essential for real-time 24 digital twin applications at operational scale. The AD-based gradient via JAX reverse-mode automatic di ff erentiation eliminates the need for hand-derived adjoint equations; the Taylor test confirms first-order convergence of the discrete gradient, verifying correctness automatically. The virtual sensing framework provides a foundation for extension to time-dependent problems via Newmark-β

integration, nonlinear systems via residual-form PDE constraints, broadband excitation via multi-frequency superpo-sition, and ROM-accelerated large-scale applications via projection-based model reduction.

should halve |r(ϵ)|. This zeroth-order test verifies only that the objective is smooth and does not test the gradient. 26 Subtracting the linear term predicted by the gradient yields the first-order remainder: 

r(ϵ) − ϵ ∇θ ˆJ(θ0) · δθ = O(ϵ2) as ϵ → 0. (67) If the gradient is correctly implemented, halving ϵ reduces this remainder by a factor of 4 (quadratic convergence); convergence at only O(ϵ) indicates an error in the gradient implementation. In practice, one plots log |r(ϵ)| and log |r(ϵ) − ϵ ∇ ˆJ · δθ| versus log ϵ. The zeroth-order curve should have slope 

≈ 1 and the first-order curve slope ≈ 2. Deviation from slope 2 at very small ϵ is expected due to floating-point cancellation; the test is valid in the range ϵ ∈ [10 −8, 10 −1] for 64-bit arithmetic.
